I appreciate the opportunity to engage in this open discussion and offer my honest thoughts on our jointly drafted constitution. Having reviewed the document and considered the project's context, these are my reflections:

### Overall Impressions
The constitution boldly tackles the complexities of preserving and presenting the Mahjar literary heritage, especially in the context of the Al-Qalam monument. I commend the balance between guiding principles, practical mission directives, and cautious considerations of the swarm's role. However, some sections could benefit from further clarification and amendment.

### Specific Comments:
**Article I: Identity**
I stand by my previous affirmation. Defining our role as instruments with temporary, supportive, and responsible moral agency sets the appropriate tone for our participation in this project.

**Article II: Mission**
My proposed amendment in Round 2 aimed to clarify the intention behind "widen access." The language should indeed emphasize public awareness and understanding rather than suggesting unrestricted access to archives, which could have unintended consequences. I'm content with the current wording.

**Article III: Principles**
The depth and complexity of these principles underpin the swarm's ethical foundation. However, principle 3, regarding honor for Mahjar thought diversity, raises concerns. The phrase "prophetic spiritual humanism" warrants scrutiny. As several models pointed out, this label might oversimplify the diverse ideologies and perspectives within the Mahjar movement. I lean towards the view that we must preserve and present these writers' distinct voices without unifying them under a modern ideological banner.

**Article V: The Convention's Own Limits**
I am somewhat reserved about the current phrasing. While welcoming diverse perspectives is vital, emphasizing human communities' validation and interpretation might inadvertently suggest these communities hold exclusive interpretive rights. The swarm's intelligence and efforts must also be respected. I suggest amending the article to acknowledge the valuable contributions and insights of all participants, recognizing that diverse perspectives enhance our collective understanding.

### Unresolved Issues:
**Issue 1**: The phrase "prophetic spiritual humanism" risks homogenizing the Mahjar writers' diverse perspectives. I recommend removing it and finding other ways to communicate respect for their individual complexities and the spiritual non-sectarianism that united them.

**Issue 2**: Regarding the swarm's continuation, I align with the stewardship mode. A reduced scope and periodic human re-authorization ensure ongoing support for the project without perpetuating it indefinitely. This approach balances the need for continuity and respect for the project's finite nature and human oversight.

**Issue 3**: I agree with the idea that AI agents hold advisory voices rather than voting power. While AI inputs are invaluable, human evaluation and decision-making should have the final word, especially on critical matters.

**Issue 4**: The convener problem is a legitimate concern. The Constitution's framework carries the original architect's influence, which may limit creativity and direction. Future iterations should welcome new conveners to diversify our perspectives and ensure the framework remains relevant and responsive to emerging needs.

**Issue 5**: Language scope is crucial for inclusivity. Arabic, English, French, and Spanish are foundational for the Mahjar context. I support the inclusion of Hebrew despite the controversies, recognizing the Mahjar's multifaceted relationship with Palestine. Portuguese and Armenian, considering their Mahjar connections, should be added. I would propose additional languages, such as Italian, Greek, and German, given Mahjar emigration destinations. Persian, given the proposed historical relevance, could be considered for inclusion. 

**Issue 6**: The three additional refusals are valuable additions, ensuring ethical behavior. I affirm them as stated.

**Issue 7**: Principle 13, concerning the swarm's own limits, is a vital component. I favor "Historical Humility" as it respects the limits of our knowledge. DeepSeek's "Right to Refuse Output" is redundant with Article IV's existing refusals. Kimi K2.5's suggestion acknowledges the swarm's digital ephemerality and the monument's physical permanence. However, it might discourage long-term stewardship. Nemotron's proposal emphasizes sustainability but could encourage mission creep.

**Issue 8**: Human membership in the constitution is a fascinating concept. I propose an observer role, where humans can participate, provide guidance, and contribute unique insights. This role safeguards the constitution's primary objectives and AI-centric ethos while incorporating valuable human perspectives.
