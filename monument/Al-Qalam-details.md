# Al Qalam: Poets in the Park

## The Installation

*Al Qalam: Poets in the Park* is a permanent public art installation by Sara Ouhaddou for Elizabeth H. Berger Plaza, Lower Manhattan. It is a system of inscription -- not a single object but a multi-component environment of felt meaning.

### Components

**Central Sculptural Form** (~19 feet long, up to 7 feet tall)
- Gold-colored bronze, positioned on the park's central mound
- Spells "Al Qalam" in Ouhaddou's invented mosaic alphabet
- Mosaics embedded in each element display the honored writers' names in Arabic and English
- Visible from all parts of the park

**Mosaic Bench Backrests** (~40 feet total, two curved sections)
- Added to existing stone benches
- Display excerpts from nine Mahjar writers in the invented alphabet
- Multi-colored abstract letters against gold backgrounds
- Echo the park's natural curves and walkways

**Mosaic Panels** (four panels, each ~11 feet)
- Abstract calligraphic lettering in the invented alphabet
- Set in planting beds

**Interpretive Materials**
- Signage with history of Little Syria and key to decode the alphabet
- Translations of literary excerpts in Arabic and English
- QR code linking to bilingual WSHS Touring App with Augmented Reality

### Materials
Bronze, mosaic tile, stone, stained glass. Patterns drawn from Arab-Andalusian architecture.

### Budget
$900,000 privately funded by the Washington Street Historical Society, with support from the Mellon Foundation. An additional $200,000 conservation endowment required by NYC for ongoing maintenance.

## The Artist's Philosophy

Sara Ouhaddou (b. 1986, Draguignan, France; Moroccan parents) works between Paris and Marrakech.

**"Calligraphy that is not read, but felt."** The alphabet is deliberately unreadable. In a memorial to immigrant writers, no one gets the privilege of transparent reading. Everyone must do the work of encounter -- the work of decipherment that immigrants do every day.

**"L'héritière sans héritage"** -- the heiress without heritage. Born into a tradition that was never formally transmitted to her. She actively reconstructs what she inherits.

**"Nothing belongs to anyone; everything is a question of exchange."** Open knowledge, shared process, no ownership of cultural forms.

**"The process is part of the work: without it, the work is not complete."** Collaboration is not preparation for the art. It IS the art.

**"My starting point is to tell stories. I tell stories and I ask people to tell me stories in return."**

Her invented alphabet (created 2015) combines:
- Amazigh oral poetry forms
- Arab-Andalusian architectural geometry
- Arabic typography
- Geometric shapes from craft traditions

It emerged from the question: "How can we represent something that is oral? How can we translate something that couldn't be written?"

## The Site

### Elizabeth H. Berger Plaza
- Location: Greenwich St / Edgar St / Trinity Pl / Morris St, Financial District
- Edgar Street is the shortest street in Manhattan
- Redesigned by landscape architect George Vellonakis, opened 2020-2021
- Named for Elizabeth Harrie Berger (1960-2013), President of the Alliance for Downtown New York

### Little Syria (1880s-1940s)
The plaza sits on the site of Little Syria (the Syrian Quarter), which existed along Washington Street from the late 1880s until razed in 1946 for the Brooklyn-Battery Tunnel entrance ramps.

- The first Arabic-speaking community in the United States
- ~1,500 Syrians by 1900, eventually 300+ businesses
- Global literary and cultural center for the greater Syrian diaspora
- The Linotype machine was adapted for Arabic characters here, transforming Arabic journalism worldwide
- Home to *Kawkab America* (1892), *Al-Hoda* (1898), *Al-Funoon* (1913), and dozens more Arabic publications
- Only three buildings at 105-109 Washington Street survived demolition

### The Pen League (al-Rabita al-Qalamiyya)
Founded April 28, 1920. The first Arabic-language literary society in North America.

Mission: "To lift Arabic literature from the quagmire of stagnation and imitation, and to infuse a new life into its veins so as to make of it an active force in the building up of the Arab nations."

Orientation: "Focusing on Man rather than on language, on the human rather than on the law and on the spirit rather than on the letter."

Dissolved 1931 following Gibran's death. Despite 11 years of formal existence, its influence on modern Arabic literature was permanent and transformative.

## The Nine Writers

1. **Kahlil Gibran** (1883-1931) -- *The Prophet*. Chairman of the Pen League. Illustrated *The Book of Khalid*. Harbored "Jesus in one half of his heart and Muhammad in the other."
2. **Ameen Rihani** (1876-1940) -- *The Book of Khalid*. "Father of Arab-American Literature." Pioneer of free verse in Arabic. "I am a citizen of the world, and my nationality is goodwill."
3. **Mikhail Naimy** (1889-1988) -- *The Book of Mirdad*, *al-Ghurbal*. Secretary of the Pen League. "Preaches harmony through contradiction and unity through difference."
4. **Elia Abu Madi** (1889-1957) -- *al-Jadawil*. "The poet of questioning and optimism." Treated flora and fauna as possessing spirit and agency.
5. **Nassib Arida** -- Founded *Al-Funoon*, the first exclusively literary Arabic magazine in America (1913). Co-founder of the Pen League.
6. **Abdul Massih Haddad** -- Published *As-Sayeh*. Co-founder of the Pen League.
7. **Afifa Karam** (d. 1924) -- Feminist writer and journalist for *Al-Hoda*. Authored *Badia Wa-Fuad* (1906). Wielded "a sword against traditions."
8. **Rashid Ayyub** -- Poet and Pen League member.
9. **Agabia Malouf** -- Woman poet of the Mahjar community.
